[Information Architecture]

[First Information Architecture Diagram]

Given the five page specifications, I designed this documented process page to fall under the portfolio navigation link.
With respect to Brown's Principles of Information Architecture, these navigation categories adhere to the principle of growth, which is the practice of creating directories broad enough to account for any future content growth. This allows any future implementations to easily add content without restructuring the entire website navigation header. This includes any upcoming projects that can easily be added to the Portfolio page, as well as updating biographies and contact links.
It also complies with the principle of front doors, which Brown defines as the idea that at least half of the site's visitors will not come through the main home page. As such, it is important to ensure that all important pages are accessible at all times. This is evident in the global navigation header, which allows all users to navigate to their desired page regardless of the current page being visited.

The first four categories (Home, Portfolio, Resume, Contact) are global structural navigation links, and the Project Website page is considered as a local structural category.

[Revised IA Diagram]

I realized that adding an images page that features notable photos (taken by myself) would be better than showing just one photo on the portfolio page, and so the image category was created. This is where my most notable photographs would be displayed in as a gallery. This also allows for Brown's principle of growth, as photos can easily be added to the end of the galley in the future.
After deciding to add an images page, I changed the Information Architecture to include the Image page. This is not a major change, but crucial to understanding the final product navigation.

Images category is now under Portfolio, which reflects in the final product.

[Wireframes]

Taking elements from the research stage, several variations of pages were initialized as wireframes. All of these ideas stemmed from the goal of creating an astronomic like site, with elements of computer science and constellations.

In regards to Christopher Wickens' 13 Principles of Display Design, the wireframe follows several principles of usability. For instance, all the global navigation headers are boldfaced and uppercase, which distinguish it from other types of text and adheres to the principle of making displays legible. To add, the principle of avoiding similarity and absolute judgement is used in the use of both text transformation, weight, and colour contrast.

[Designing Website and Other References]

From the prototype, the website was implemented in accordance. This site naturally follows from and displays the aforementioned research and design processes.
It is important to note that there are several changes made to the final product that were not documented in the prototype. However, most of these modifications are impartial to the overall structure of the site, and any major differences are explicitly described here.

In error, I initially implemented the website without accounting for the different containers required. Each individual text and image had to be padded with the same number of pixels, rather than simply padding a single container that held all the texts and photos. To add, the site was explicitly formatted for a standard 1280x720px screen, but outside the scope of this project, I realized that this site should be accessible regardless of screen resolution. This made the stylesheet and html files unorganized and long to manage, and so this was scrapped entirely.

After starting again, I took necessary containers and position formatting into account. This included creating containers with borders first, and adjusting their margins using percentages of the user’s screen resolution instead of pixels.

A reference to how the border element was used to format the home page. Note that the image currently pictured to the right belongs to @1L9l2Aa8UCL0IGJ on Twitter, and was only a temporary placeholder for any picture. It remains available in the source files for reference.

To make the background, I used ibisPaint X, a drawing software, to draw the space background and the moving starlink found in the home page. Then, I used Adobe After Effects to create the movie, which can be downloaded here.
I also created another loop video with a different gradient background, that can also be downloaded here, but in terms of design, the first video is significantly more appealing to me, and so this video only remains for initial documentation.

[Accessibility]

Many steps were taken to ensure that accessibility was available on this website. This includes using the WAVE Evaluation tool found on the Google Chrome extension store, which shows a 17.82:1 contrast ratio on this site, passing the WAGC2.0 Accessibility Guidelines. For reference, the foreground includes all text on this site, and the background colour is derived from the videos and photos created.
